    • Poisonous mushrooms

    Amanita phalloides (Death Cap Mushroom):

    • Where it grows: It's mostly found in deciduous forests but can also appear under conifers.
    • When it grows: Usually seen in spring and autumn but can be found in different seasons.
    • Edibility: Extremely poisonous and potentially deadly if eaten. Not edible.
    • Fun fact: Its toxin, amanitin, is one of the most potent among poisonous mushrooms and
      can cause irreversible damage to the liver and other internal organs.

    Amanita muscaria (Fly Agaric Mushroom):

    • Where it grows: Mostly found in both deciduous and coniferous forests, often under birch and fir trees.
    • When it grows: Appears in summer and autumn but can also be found in spring.
    • Edibility: Considered non-edible and potentially poisonous if consumed in large quantities.
    • Fun fact: It has a long association with culture and mythology, often linked to shamanic rituals and folklore.

    Galerina marginata (Deadly Galerina Mushroom):

    • Where it grows: Mainly found in deciduous forests, often near decaying logs and forest debris.
    • When it grows: More common in autumn but can also be found in other seasons.
    • Edibility: Extremely poisonous and can cause severe liver and kidney damage if ingested
    • Fun fact: Its appearance closely resembles that of other edible mushrooms,
      making it particularly dangerous for inexperienced mushroom foragers.

    Cortinarius orellanus (Orellani Cortinarius Mushroom):

    • Where it grows: Common in coniferous forests in Europe.
    • When it grows: Mainly found in autumn.
    • Edibility: Extremely poisonous and can cause severe kidney damage or even death.
    • Fun fact: Also known as the "scalp cap mushroom" due to its resemblance to the edible Cortinarius caperatus.

    Inocybe geophylla (White Fibercap Mushroom):

    • Where it grows: Found in meadows, forests, and parks, often near decomposing plant debris.
    • When it grows: Common in autumn.
    • Edibility: Some species of Inocybe contain muscarine, making them non-edible and potentially poisonous.
    • Fun fact: The Inocybe genus is highly diverse, with many species that can be difficult to distinguish
      even for experienced mycologists.
    Edible mushrooms

    Porcini Mushrooms (Boletus edulis):

    .
    • Where it grows: Mostly in forests with broadleaf and conifer trees, often near oak, beech, and pine trees.
    • When it grows: Most common in fall, especially after rains.
    • Edibility: Porcini mushrooms are tasty and often used in cooking. But be careful when picking them because
      some similar mushrooms are poisonous.
    • Fun Fact: Porcini mushrooms have a unique and delicious smell, making them popular in kitchens worldwide.
      They're sometimes called the "king of mushrooms" because they're so highly regarded.

    Honey Fungus (Armillaria mellea):

    • Where it grows: On dead wood, like tree stumps and fallen logs.
    • When it grows: Usually in autumn.
    • Edibility: Honey fungi are edible and tasty. They can be eaten raw or cooked and have a mild flavor.
    • Fun Fact: Honey fungi are known for their ability to form long networks of tiny threads called mycelium,
      which can stretch for miles underground.

    Chanterelle Mushrooms (Cantharellus cibarius):

    • Where it grows: In forests with conifer and broadleaf trees, but also in meadows and open fields.
    • When it grows: Most common in summer and fall.
    • Edibility: Chanterelles are prized for their delicate flavor and meaty texture.
      They're delicious when cooked with butter and garlic.
    • Fun Fact: Chanterelles can form a special relationship with certain trees,
      helping them absorb more nutrients from the soil.

    Wood Ear Mushrooms (Auricularia auricula-judae):

    • Where it grows: On decomposing wood, like dead trees and fallen branches.
    • When it grows: Can be found all year round in temperate climates.
    • Edibility: Wood ear mushrooms are edible and often used in Asian cuisine.
      They have a gelatinous texture and can be added to soups and stir-fries.
    • Fun Fact: They're called "wood ear" because they look like ears growing on wood.
      They're also known as "Judas's ear" in some legends.
